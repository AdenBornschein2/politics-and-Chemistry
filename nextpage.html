Ion
    an atom with a charge
    the number of protons in unequal to the number of electrons
    example negitive electron 2 protons 3 electrons
    Postive ion 3 protons 2 electrons
Cations
    Positively charged atoms(more protons)
    Occur whenever atoms lose electrons
    Metals tend to form cations
Anions:
    Negatively charged atoms(more electrons)
    occur whenever atoms gain electrons
      End in -ide
      nonmetals tend to form anions
    Ionic Compounds
      involve ionic bonding = the transfer of electrons from one atom to another in order to comply with the octet rule.
    Transition Elements and Roman Numerals
      Cations receive roman numerals when they can take on more than one ionic form
      If the cation has a roman numeral after it, that is it’s charge
      Ex: Iron(II) = Fe+2
    ionic compound formulas
      Example: Magnesium Chloride
      Write the element symbols
      Mg Cl
      (cation first, then anion)
      Write the charges
      Mg+2 Cl-1
      IGNORE signs, 1s and duplicates
      Mg2Cl
      Swap and Drop the numbers
      MgCl2
    Polyatomic Anions
      2 or more atoms bonded together, creating a negatively charged molecule
      End in –ate or –ite (w/few exceptions)
      Treat them as a single unit!
    Naming Ionic Compounds with Polyatomic Anions
      Same as other ionic compounds
      KNO3
      Cation, then anion found on Common Ions reference page
    ionic compounds
      Write the element symbols
      (cation first, then anion)
      Write the charges
      IGNORE charges, 1s and duplicates
      Swap and Drop the numbers
    Writing Formulas with Polyatomic Anions
      Same as other ionic compounds PLUS
      Zinc Sulfate
      Place the polyatomic anion in parentheses and do nothing with the subscript inside.
      The charge goes outside of the parentheses.
      Dol
      Tin(II) bicarbonate Sn(HCO3)2
      Aluminum Phosphide AlP
      Cu2SO4 Copper (I) Sulfate
      FeO Iron (II) Oxide
